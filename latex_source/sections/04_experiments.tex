\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsubsection{Datasets}

We evaluate on three public tabular IDS benchmarks.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{NSL-KDD} \cite{tavallaee2009nslkdd}: \texttt{KDDTrain+} (125,973 records) and \texttt{KDDTest+} (22,544 records), 41 input features.
    \item \textbf{UNSW-NB15} \cite{moustafa2015unswnb15}: official training split (175,341 records) and testing split (82,332 records), 42 features after removing identifier columns.
    \item \textbf{CIC-IDS2017} \cite{sharafaldin2018cicids2017}: 78 features after preprocessing. We use random stratified split as the main evaluation protocol and the stricter day-based split as a supplementary generalization test.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Baselines}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Vanilla PPO}: single-agent RL without adversarial training.
    \item \textbf{MARL}: simultaneous attacker-defender updates without bilevel nesting.
    \item \textbf{Transformer-IDS}: FT-Transformer backbone \cite{gorishniy2021fttransformer} trained on clean data only (no adversarial training). Serves as the direct ablation baseline for the BiAT framework.
    \item \textbf{LSTM-IDS}: two-layer LSTM supervised baseline \cite{staudemeyer2019lstm}.
    \item \textbf{HGBT-IDS}: histogram gradient boosting on tabular features.
    \item \textbf{XGBoost-IDS} and \textbf{LightGBM-IDS}: modern boosted-tree baselines.
    \item \textbf{BiAT-MLP} and \textbf{BiAT-FTTransformer}: supervised detectors trained with the BiAT (Bilevel Adversarial Training) framework.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Training Details}

All results report means over four random seeds (42, 123, 3407, 8888). NSL-KDD uses 100 RL episodes. On UNSW-NB15, $K_{\text{inner}} = 1$ to stabilize RL training. BiAT-FTTransformer uses $d_{\text{token}} = 64$, 3 transformer layers, 8 attention heads, AdamW ($\text{lr} = 7 \times 10^{-4}$, $\text{wd} = 10^{-4}$), $\lambda = 0.6$, PGD inner loop ($\epsilon = 0.02$, $\alpha = 0.005$, 2 steps).

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Recall}: $TP/(TP+FN)$; \textbf{Precision}: $TP/(TP+FP)$
    \item \textbf{F1-score}: harmonic mean; \textbf{FPR}: $FP/(FP+TN)$
\end{itemize}

        \bottomrule

The robustness results reveal two key findings.

\textbf{Finding 1 (NSL-KDD RL models):} All RL-based models collapse under FGSM-$\epsilon$0.3 (FPR$\to$100\%), confirming that the RL bilevel training alone is insufficient to provide strong adversarial robustness against gradient-based attacks.

\textbf{Finding 2 (BiAT adversarial training benefit):} On UNSW-NB15, Transformer-IDS (no adversarial training) and BiAT-FTTransformer have nearly identical clean FPR (15.17\% vs.\ 14.97\%), but under FGSM-$\epsilon$0.3 their FPR diverges substantially: 70.86\% vs.\ 54.56\%---a 16.3 percentage-point improvement from bilevel adversarial training. The same trend appears under PGD: BiAT-FTTransformer reaches 73.43\% / 96.56\% FPR at $\epsilon=0.1/0.3$, while BiAT-MLP degrades to 51.83\% / 100.00\%. This confirms that the BiAT framework's benefit is primarily in adversarial conditions rather than clean-data accuracy, consistent with the robustness-accuracy tradeoff established by adversarial training theory \cite{zhang2019trades, madry2018pgd}.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Sensitivity}
\label{sec:hyperparam}

\textbf{$K_{\text{inner}}$ on NSL-KDD.}
Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity_kinner} reports the $K_{\text{inner}}$ sweep over $\{1, 3, 5, 10\}$ with 2 seeds. F1 peaks at $K_{\text{inner}} = 5$ (84.10\%) and degrades at $K_{\text{inner}} = 10$ (80.02\%), likely due to attacker over-optimization. The default $K_{\text{inner}} = 5$ is validated as the best choice.

\textbf{$\lambda$ (adversarial weight) on UNSW-NB15.}
We additionally swept $\lambda \in \{0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9\}$ for BiAT-FTTransformer on UNSW-NB15. The quantitative results show a monotonic degradation as $\lambda$ increases: F1 drops from 88.33\% ($\lambda=0.3$) to 86.11\% ($\lambda=0.9$), while FPR rises from 20.08\% to 32.91\%. Thus, smaller-to-moderate adversarial weights are preferable in this setting, whereas overly large adversarial emphasis harms clean-performance operating points. The detailed sweep is provided in \texttt{sensitivity\_lambda\_unsw\_nb15.csv}.

\subsection{Discussion}

Four main conclusions emerge from the experiments.

\textbf{(1) Bi-ARL provides a principled RL formulation:} On NSL-KDD, bilevel Stackelberg training yields the best RL F1 (80.17\%, Cohen's $d=1.66$ vs.\ vanilla PPO) and reduces FPR by 29.16 pp, confirming the value of leader-follower optimization over simultaneous update.

\textbf{(2) BiAT-FTTransformer is the strongest neural detector on UNSW-NB15:} It achieves the lowest neural FPR (14.97\%) while trailing LightGBM by only 2.35 F1 points. The Transformer-IDS baseline confirms that the bilevel adversarial training contributes beyond backbone choice alone, particularly in adversarial conditions.

\textbf{(3) BiAT adversarial training robustness tradeoff:} Consistent with TRADES theory \cite{zhang2019trades}, BiAT-FTTransformer sacrifices $\approx$1\% clean F1 vs.\ Transformer-IDS but reduces FGSM-$\epsilon$0.3 FPR by 16.3 pp (70.86\%$\to$54.56\%). This tradeoff is the core contribution of the BiAT framework.

\textbf{(4) Tree-based methods dominate overall F1:} LightGBM leads UNSW-NB15 and nearly saturates CIC-IDS2017, motivating future work combining bilevel adversarial training with ensemble methods.
